Coming Soon: The Hedron, The First Manifold Rollercoaster

We all know of the miricals of Geometric Spatial Gluing, or GSG for short. The technology that links separate spaces together as one and allows us to rewrite the laws of physics. Being the greatest advancement of the 24th century, GSG technology has revolutionized many fields from architecure to transportaion. Geoland has finally released it's newest attraction, The Hedron, which uses GSG technology to transform the experience of a rollercoaster.

The Hedron Takes place in what is known as a manifold, a mostly enclosed with walls that have been spatially glued. The border of the Hedron is a simple manifold. A cube that glues tangent walls together while simultaneously glueing the top and bottom. The ride can support itself under these conditions thanks to the tracks being made of Mobius metal, which is immune to GSG's spatial manipulation.

The riders of the Hedron will spend the entire ride inside a space of 100 cubic feet. To the rider however, the Hedron feels like the largest rollercoaster in the world. Being "confined" to a manifold gives the ride seemingly infinite possibilities for the path the riders take.

A word from Dhomas Toyle, thoughts on the Hedron and the future of manifold rollercoasters

Dhomas Toyle is a leading researcher in applying GSG technology to architecture, and seems to be very excited about what the Hedron could mean for the future of amusemtnt park rides

" There have been other attractions that have used some level of spatial gluing in certain areas, but the Hedron is the first roller coaster to ever encase the full ride in a manifold. The Hedron is currently not a very long ride because of it’s experimental nature, but it shows off the limitless potential GSG technology could have for other rides. Why travel a great distance upward just to drop downward half the distance for speed. Instead, a ride with GSG plating could have the top and bottom of it’s border glued, meaning that the cart could travel downward from any point in the ride with no elevation, keep going down as long as it wants to, building speed after each time it reappears at the top What if some sides are glued with a quarter rotation, changing the rider’s perspective when crossing into a new side. Imagine what could happen if the cart travels through an edge or corner of the manifold. (actually don’t imagine that. This might be a potentially disastrous safety concern.). The manifold could even be constructed using different polyhedra in the future. There could be quite the experience had from just traveling in a straight line on a dodecahedron with a ⅕ rotation and ending up on different sides each time. GSG technology has opened doors no one even thought existed. " - Dhomas Toyle
